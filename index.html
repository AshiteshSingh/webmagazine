Saint Thérèse: The Little Flower

"I will spend my heaven doing good on earth"

Youngest Doctor of the Church

Youngest Doctor of the Church
0:00
1:30

Saint Thérèse is considered the youngest Doctor of the Church, having died at age 24 and been named a Doctor of the Church posthumously by Pope John Paul II in 1997. Only the third woman to receive this honor.

The Little Way

The Little Way
0:00
1:45

St. Thérèse developed "The Little Way" - a spiritual path emphasizing performing everyday, ordinary actions with great love, trusting in God's infinite mercy, and accepting one's "littleness" as means of spiritual growth.

Early Life & Family

Early Life & Family
0:00
1:20

Born Marie Françoise-Thérèse Martin on January 2, 1873, in Alençon, France. She was the youngest of nine children, four of whom became Carmelite nuns. Her parents, Louis and Zélie Martin, were both declared saints in 2015.

Carmelite Nun at 15

Carmelite Nun at 15
0:00
1:10

Despite her young age, Thérèse entered the Carmelite convent at Lisieux at age 15 in 1888. She joined her older sisters Pauline and Marie, and later her sister Céline also joined the same convent.

The Story of a Soul

The Story of a Soul
0:00
1:35

Her autobiography "The Story of a Soul" was published after her death and became one of the most widely read spiritual autobiographies. It spread her "Little Way" teachings across the world and contributed to her rapid canonization.

Death & Canonization

Death & Canonization
0:00
1:25

Died of tuberculosis on September 30, 1897, at age 24. Canonized by Pope Pius XI in 1925, just 28 years after her death. Her last words were "My God, I love you." She promised to "spend my heaven doing good on earth."

Mahatma Gandhi: The Great Soul

"Be the change you wish to see in the world"

International Day of Non-Violence

International Day of Non-Violence
0:00
1:40

In recognition of his philosophy of non-violence (Ahimsa) and civil disobedience (Satyagraha), the United Nations General Assembly declared October 2nd as the International Day of Non-Violence in 2007.

Nobel Peace Prize Nominations

Nobel Peace Prize Nominations
0:00
1:25

Mahatma Gandhi was nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize five times (1937, 1938, 1939, 1947, 1948) but never received the award, a decision that has been widely considered a historical oversight.

Early Life & Education

Born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, Gujarat. Studied law in London and was called to the bar at age 22. Moved to South Africa in 1893 where he first developed his philosophy of non-violent resistance.

The Dandi Salt March

The Dandi Salt March
0:00
1:50

Led the famous 400 km Salt March from Ashram Ahmedabad to Dandi in 1930, protesting the British salt monopoly. This 24-day march became a pivotal moment in India's independence movement.

Philosophy of Satyagraha

Philosophy of Satyagraha
0:00
1:35

Developed the concept of Satyagraha (truth force) - non-violent resistance based on truth, courage, and non-violence. This philosophy influenced leaders worldwide including Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela.

Assassination & Legacy

Assassination & Legacy
0:00
1:45

Assassinated on January 30, 1948, by Nathuram Godse. Despite his tragic end, Gandhi's principles of non-violence, truth, and justice continue to inspire movements for peace and freedom worldwide.

Dussehra: The Festival of Victory

"Victory of Good over Evil"

A Celebration of a Goddess's Triumph

A Celebration of a Goddess's Triumph
0:00
2:15

In many parts of India, particularly in the eastern and southern states, Dussehra, also known as Vijayadashami, is not primarily about Rama's victory. It commemorates the triumph of Goddess Durga over the buffalo demon Mahishasura.

The Pandavas' Agyatvas and the Shami Tree

The Pandavas' Agyatvas and the Shami Tree
0:00
2:30

A fascinating legend from the Mahabharata is associated with Dussehra. After completing their twelve years of exile, the Pandavas had to spend a year in incognito (agyaatvas). They hid their divine weapons in a Shami tree before entering the kingdom of Virata. On Vijayadashami, they retrieved their weapons, worshipped the tree, and revealed their true identity.

In Their Quiet Way

A tribute to Gandhi and Saint Thérèse of Lisieux

They did not lead with power, they led with presence. They did not sit on thrones, they knelt in silence. A rosary… a spinning wheel… threads and thorns, patience and prayer. She loved in silence, he fasted in truth. She bore the hidden flame, he bore the steadfast light. Without lifting a weapon, without raising a voice, they changed the world. Without pride, without noise, they changed the world. Their lives were answers, not arguments. Their days were prayers, not proclamations. Their steps were songs, not speeches. And in the hush they left behind, we still find— the fragrance of faith, the whisper of hope, the eternal shape of love.
